## 📚 Theory

### The confusion matrix

For binary classification (fraud = the positive class):

|  | Predicted: fraud | Predicted: not fraud |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Actually fraud** | TP (caught) | **FN (missed a fraudster)** |
| **Actually not fraud** | **FP (blocked an honest user)** | TN |

Two errors — two different damages:
- **FN** — missed fraud: the company loses money directly.
- **FP** — a blocked honest customer: ruined experience, support calls, churn.

### The core metrics

| Metric | Formula | Answers the question |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Accuracy** | (TP+TN)/all | "What share of predictions is correct?" — misleading under imbalance |
| **Precision** | TP/(TP+FP) | "Of those flagged as fraud — how many really are?" |
| **Recall** | TP/(TP+FN) | "Of all fraud — how much did we catch?" |
| **F1** | harmonic mean of P and R | a compromise when both matter |

**Precision and recall are always in tension:** lower the threshold → catch more fraud (recall ↑), but block honest users more often (precision ↓).

### ROC-AUC and PR-AUC

- **ROC curve:** TPR (recall) against FPR across all thresholds. **AUC** is the area under it: the probability that a random fraud case gets a higher score than a random honest one. 0.5 — a coin flip, 1.0 — ideal.
- ROC-AUC is threshold-independent and handy for comparing models, **but** under heavy imbalance it can look optimistic.
- **PR-AUC** (precision-recall) is more honest with a rare positive class — use it for fraud, defects, rare diseases.

### How to choose a metric: from the cost of errors

The mature engineer's algorithm:

1. Write down the cost of an FP and an FN in money/consequences.
2. Understand the operational constraint (how many cases can the manual review team handle?).
3. Choose the metric: e.g. "recall at precision ≥ 90%" or "precision within the top-1000 daily alerts".

## 💻 Practice

Take the churn model from module 5 and look at it properly.

```python
from sklearn.metrics import (confusion_matrix, precision_score, recall_score,
                             roc_auc_score, average_precision_score,
                             precision_recall_curve)

pred_05 = (proba >= 0.5).astype(int)
print(confusion_matrix(y_test, pred_05))
# [[36214   306]
#  [ 2101  1379]]   ← at the 0.5 threshold we miss 2101 of 3480 churners (recall 40%)

print(f"precision={precision_score(y_test, pred_05):.2f}  recall={recall_score(y_test, pred_05):.2f}")
# precision=0.82  recall=0.40

print(f"ROC-AUC = {roc_auc_score(y_test, proba):.3f}")            # 0.834
print(f"PR-AUC  = {average_precision_score(y_test, proba):.3f}")  # 0.581
```

### Fitting the threshold to the business

Retention can handle 5000 calls; each retained customer brings $150, a call costs $2.

```python
prec, rec, thr = precision_recall_curve(y_test, proba)
# find the threshold that puts ~5000 test customers into alerts
# → threshold 0.31: precision 0.55, recall 0.79
# Economics: 5000 calls * $2 = $10K; retained ≈ 0.55*5000*0.3 (call conversion) * $150 ≈ $124K
```

At the 0.5 threshold we "saved" on calls but lost 60% of churners. Threshold 0.31 is worse on precision — and **better on money**.

2. The right toolkit for anti-fraud: confusion matrix + PR-AUC + "recall at a given precision"; the threshold comes from the economics.
3. Always compare the model with a **dumb baseline** (constant, random): if it doesn't win — there is no model.

## 📌 Remember

- Accuracy lies under class imbalance.
- Precision = alert purity; Recall = catch completeness; they always trade off.
- ROC-AUC — model comparison; PR-AUC — honesty with a rare class.
- The threshold is chosen from error costs and operational limits, not "0.5 by default".

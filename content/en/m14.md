## 📚 Theory

### Time series components

- **Trend** — the long-term direction (the store is growing).
- **Seasonality** — repeating cycles: weekly (weekend peaks), yearly (Christmas trees in December).
- **Events** — holidays, promos, price changes.
- **Noise** — the unexplained part.

### Naive baselines — respect them

1. **Naive:** tomorrow = today.
2. **Seasonal naive:** next Wednesday = last Wednesday.
3. **Moving average** over 4 weeks.

In retail, seasonal naive often delivers 80% of a complex model's quality. A model that can't beat it is wasted time.

### Two approaches to forecasting

1. **Classic series models** (ARIMA, ETS, Prophet): good for a single series with clear structure.
2. **ML on tabular features** (boosting): turn the series into a table of "features → next period's value". For **thousands of SKUs** with one model — the industry standard (this is how the M5 competition was won).

### Features for the ML approach

- **Lags:** sales 1, 7, 14, 28 days ago.
- **Rolling statistics:** mean/median/std over 7/28 days (⚠️ the window ends **before** the forecast day).
- **Calendar:** day of week, month, holidays, days to/after a holiday.
- **SKU attributes and events:** category, price, promo flag.

### Validation: forward only

Random K-fold is forbidden: the model would see the future. Use a **rolling/expanding window**: train up to T → forecast [T, T+4 weeks] → shift → repeat. This mimics real usage.

Metrics: MAE/WAPE (error as a share of volume) — clear to purchasing; MAPE is dangerous with zero sales.

The key line is `g.shift(1).rolling(win)`: shift first, then the window, so the rolling mean **does not include the current day** (otherwise leakage).

<details>
<summary><b>Review with Lena</b></summary>

1. **Forecasting 28 days ahead** means production won't have lag-1 for the distant days. Honest schemes: recursive forecasting (predict day by day, feeding your own forecasts back) or direct models per horizon. Make sure test features are built the same way they will be in battle.
2. New SKUs without history — forecast by category analogues (cold start again, as in module 13).
3. Purchasing often needs a **quantile** forecast, not a point one ("with 90% probability sales won't exceed X") — LightGBM supports `objective="quantile"`. Safety stock is computed from a quantile, not from the mean.
4. The promo flag is the strongest feature: without it the model "explains" promo spikes as seasonality and overpredicts regular days.
</details>

## 📌 Remember

- Seasonal naive first; if you can't beat it — the model isn't needed.
- Lags and windows strictly from the past (`shift` before `rolling`).
- Validation — rolling over time only.
- WAPE/MAE + quantiles for inventory; mind the production forecast horizon.

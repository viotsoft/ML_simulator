## 📚 Theory

### The three families of recommenders

1. **Non-personal:** bestsellers, trends. The baseline that must be in every comparison. Often the only option for new users.
2. **Collaborative filtering (CF):** "similar users buy similar things". Works purely on behavior; needs no product descriptions. Weakness — the **cold start** (a new product/user without history).
3. **Content-based:** recommend similar by attributes (category, brand, description text, image). Solves the product cold start.

Production systems are hybrids with a **two-stage architecture**: first a light model selects hundreds of candidates (retrieval), then a heavy model ranks them precisely (ranking). That's how YouTube, Amazon and every large marketplace work.

### Matrix factorization

The "users × products" interaction matrix is gigantic and 99.99% empty. The idea: decompose it into the product of two narrow matrices — user **embeddings** `U (n×k)` and product embeddings `V (m×k)`, k ≈ 32–256. A product's score for a user = the dot product of their vectors. Similar tastes → nearby vectors.

### Explicit vs implicit feedback

- **Explicit:** 1–5 ratings. Rare in e-commerce.
- **Implicit:** views, cart, purchases. There are no "negatives": the absence of a purchase ≠ "dislike" — maybe the user just never saw it. Implicit data needs special methods (ALS with confidence weights, BPR).

### Ranking metrics

The user sees the top-K (say, 10 slots) — what matters is the **top of the list**:

- **precision@k** — the share of relevant items in the top-k;
- **recall@k** — the share of all relevant items found;
- **NDCG@k** — accounts for position: a relevant item in 1st place is worth more than in 10th;
- **coverage/novelty** — are we recommending the same things to everyone?

Validation — **by time**: train on history up to date T, evaluate on purchases after T.

## 💻 Practice: ALS on implicit data

```python
import implicit
from scipy.sparse import csr_matrix

# events: purchase weight 3, cart 2, view 1
events["weight"] = events["type"].map({"view": 1, "cart": 2, "purchase": 3})
matrix = csr_matrix((events.weight, (events.user_idx, events.item_idx)))

model = implicit.als.AlternatingLeastSquares(
    factors=64, regularization=0.05, iterations=20, random_state=42)
model.fit(matrix)

item_ids, scores = model.recommend(user_idx, matrix[user_idx],
                                   N=10, filter_already_liked_items=True)
```

<details>
<summary><b>Architecture review with Lena</b></summary>

1. **Cold start:** new users get regional bestsellers; new products get content similarity (description embeddings). A hybrid, not a single model.
2. `filter_already_liked_items` — don't recommend what's already bought. Sounds obvious, but it's the most common recommender bug in production.
3. Offline metrics are only a candidate filter: **the final decision is made by an A/B test** (module 3!) on revenue per user.
4. Beware the feedback loop: the model recommends → users click the recommended → the log confirms the model. Keep a share of traffic on random/popular recommendations for honest data.
</details>

## 📌 Remember

- The "bestsellers" baseline belongs in every comparison.
- CF learns from behavior; implicit ≠ explicit feedback — use ALS/BPR.
- Ranking metrics: NDCG@k, precision@k; time-based validation.
- Production is a hybrid + two stages (retrieval → ranking) + an A/B test.

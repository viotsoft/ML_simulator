## 📚 Theory: the 7-step framework

### 1. Clarify the requirements (never skip!)

The questions a junior doesn't ask and a middle always does:
- What's the business goal? (revenue? engagement? retention?)
- Scale: how many users/products/requests per second?
- Latency: how many ms does the feed have to build in?
- What data and infrastructure already exist?

### 2. Translate the business metric into ML metrics

The chain: business metric (revenue per session) → online proxy metrics (CTR, add-to-cart rate) → offline model metrics (NDCG@k, recall@k). Know where the chain can break: CTR grows on clickbait while revenue doesn't.

### 3. Data

Sources, volume, labeling, freshness. Also here: cold start, feedback loops, privacy.

### 4. The model: from simple to complex

Always walk the ladder: rules/top → simple model → complex. The two-stage architecture for recommendations: **retrieval** (hundreds of candidates out of millions, a light model/ANN index) → **ranking** (a heavy model precisely sorts the candidates).

### 5. The training pipeline and features

How the train set is built, how features are computed, where the **feature store** lives (the same features for training and inference — otherwise training/serving skew!), how often retraining happens.

### 6. Serving and infrastructure

Online (real-time API) vs offline (nightly recompute, serve from cache)? Latency/cost/freshness. Fallbacks for model failure.

### 7. Evaluation, rollout, monitoring

Offline evaluation → A/B test → gradual rollout (canary). Monitoring of quality, drift, business metrics; a rollback plan.

## 💻 Practice: applying it to the "smart feed"

**1. Requirements (clarified with Victor):** goal — revenue per session; 2M MAU, peak 3k RPS on the home page; the feed's latency budget — 150 ms; clickstream — 50M events/day.

**2. Metrics:** North star — revenue/session (A/B). Proxies: feed CTR, add-to-cart. Offline: recall@100 for retrieval, NDCG@10 for ranking. Guardrails: session length, return rate, feed diversity.

**3. Data:** clickstream (views/clicks/purchases), the catalog, the module-12 segments. Cold start: new users → regional bestsellers + an onboarding survey; new products → content embeddings. Feedback loop: 5% of traffic — randomized impressions for honest logs.

**4. The model:**
```
retrieval:  ALS/two-tower embeddings + ANN index (FAISS)  → 500 candidates
ranking:    gradient boosting on [user × item × context] features → top 20
the A/B baseline: the current "bestsellers"
```

**5. Pipeline:** nightly embedding recompute (Airflow); the ranking model retrains weekly; features live in the feature store, the online part (recent views) in Redis.

**6. Serving:** retrieval from a precomputed ANN index (~10 ms) + CPU ranking (~30 ms over 500 candidates) + business rules (drop purchased items, diversify categories). Fallback: on model timeout serve the cached top — the feed never goes down.

**7. Rollout:** an offline gate (NDCG@10 ≥ baseline+20%) → A/B at 5% → 50% → 100%. We monitor CTR, p99 latency, score distribution drift; rollback via a feature flag in a minute.

<details>
<summary><b>The "mean interviewer's" (Lena's) questions and answers</b></summary>

- **"Why not one big end-to-end model?"** — Ranking 300k products with a heavy model in 150 ms is impossible; the two-stage design trades completeness for speed in a controlled way.
- **"What if the A/B shows CTR up but revenue down?"** — Trust revenue (the North star). CTR is a proxy; the feed may have drifted toward cheap clickable items. Add price/margin to the ranking target.
- **"The biggest risk?"** — Training/serving skew: production features computed differently than in training. Cured by a feature store and logging the actual inference features, then training on exactly those.
- **"How do we degrade when Redis fails?"** — The feed without real-time features (daily ones only) — slightly worse quality but alive. Graceful degradation is designed in advance.
</details>

## 📌 Remember

- System design starts with questions, not architecture.
- The metric chain: business → online proxies → offline; guardrails are mandatory.
- The retrieval → ranking two-stage design is the high-load recommendation standard.
- A feature store fights training/serving skew; a fallback for every component.
